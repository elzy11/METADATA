About the author


My name is Elza Ucelniece. I am a student at the Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam studying Artificial Intelligence. My interest in that field became an interest in data. Data tells an interesting story and we can learn so much from it. Though it tends to be biased, and what matters most is who collects it, controlls it. They get to decide what the data means and what it is worth. This became a major concern as Big Tech increases its power, now with the help of AI especially. The systems are growing bigger and more complex. If we don't start seeing this and doing something about it, it will be too late to change the course. Intelligence only evolves. My main goal with this project, and potentially future ones, is to give the data back to the people. The data is there and available, so WE should collect it, use it, learn from it ourselves.

The data is not real… or rather, most of the user data in this project is calculated, estimated, and derived from real figures but difficult to verify in real time at this level of detail. This is due to the limitations of acquiring user data at such a scale. The EU GDPR does, in principle, allow me to acquire such data through legal procedures on an academic basis using the same methods, and with the analysis made anonymous. And that is essentially what Meta does, except they then label and group you, and most of all use you - sell your predicted behaviour to advertising companies. Additionally, I could have also purchased this data, but the price is high relative to the ambition of this project. I would rather not fund a system that already profits from my presence in it anyway. Therefore, my main focus was therefore the visual effect: escaping the enframing digital structures we feel trapped in by design, and demonstrating the scale of the system we have agreed to and how it profits from us. I want to portray that the issue is bigger than the individual, by showing what the collective perspective would look like.

The financial data is real. We know about the money but not the methods that make it.

About this project

Metadata Metaphors

is a bachelor's thesis project at the Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam about how Meta — Facebook and Instagram — extracts economic value from human attention, at a scale most people never see. Started as just The Attention Market and developed into more than that. Ending with Our Data Community
The project builds on three theoretical layers. Lessig argues that code is law — technical architecture regulates behaviour more effectively than any legal system, and extraction is built in by design. Zuboff describes what that architecture is used for: converting human behaviour into data, processed into predictions, sold to advertisers. Heidegger describes what it means to exist inside such a system — where everything, including people, becomes a resource waiting to be optimized.

Three visualizations give each layer a form.


The Market — the evidence. Two charts for the same company: engagement falling, revenue rising across fourteen years. The divergence is the argument.
The Tree — the mechanism. Branches as user activity above ground, roots as accumulated profit below. A pulse travels downward through the trunk whether anyone engages or not. Passive data collection does not need your click.
The Space — the condition. Each star is a real quantity of people inside the platform right now, orbiting a black hole that grows with every dollar earned since 2004. You cannot see the edge from inside it.

they ask what a policy report cannot — not just what this system does, but what it feels like to be inside it.

About the data

This is an independent academic art and research project produced at Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or connected to Meta Platforms, Inc. in any way.

Stock prices, revenue figures, and user counts are drawn from Meta's audited public filings and quarterly earnings releases. Engagement volumes are modeled, not measured — Meta does not disclose them publicly — anchored to published industry benchmarks from Hootsuite, Sprout Social, and Buffer, and marked EST throughout. A synthetic dataset adds visual texture to the candlestick charts only.

Values from 2026 onward, behavioral rates, and all derived quantities are estimates. Every visual scale is stated where it is used. Individual stars in the galaxy visualization are statistical simulations — no real person's data is shown, collected, or used.

The people are counted. The engagement is modeled.

The proposal

Abstract.
This thesis examines how interactive data visualisation can make the hidden mechanisms of social media data collection legible to ordinary users. Drawing on Zuboff’s surveillance capitalism, Lessig’s codeas-law argument, and the attention economy literature, it develops The Attention Market — a browser-based, stock-market-styled visualisation that renders aggregated social media data flows as an immersive experience. Where privacy policy and regulation have failed to make these systems felt, this project asks whether design can succeed. The methodology uses p5.js with open-source and synthetic datasets, evaluated through a small user study measuring shifts in awareness.

Privacy Protection Practices

None of these are perfect solutions. The system was not built by individual choices and cannot be dismantled by them alone — but starting somewhere matters. Share what you know, add to the list, or leave a post in the community tab.

A. individually you can

  • use less — the obvious but tricky one. Every session generates data whether you engage or not. Less time is less signal.
  • adjust your settings — disable unnecessary location tracking, review app permissions regularly, delete apps you don't use. Small reductions in data collection add up.
  • use encrypted services — Signal instead of WhatsApp, Proton Mail instead of Gmail, Firefox or Brave instead of Chrome. Business models matter: subscription services have less incentive to profile you than advertising-funded ones.
  • reject cookies — yes, manually rejecting all takes ages every time, but browser extensions like uBlock Origin or Cookie AutoDelete do it for you.
  • hide — VPNs, proxies, temporary or fake email addresses for sign-ups. Reduces your traceable footprint across platforms.
  • obfuscate — generate noise. Browser plug-ins like AdNauseam simulate random browsing and pollute your behavioral profile with fake clicks. If the signal can't be trusted, it loses value.

B. collectively we can

  • talk about it — awareness spreads through conversation, not just policy. Share what you know.
  • demand alternatives — support privacy-respecting platforms, open-source tools, and cooperative digital services. Viable alternatives to surveillance-based business models need users to exist.
  • data trusts — collective institutions that negotiate data rights on behalf of communities, like a union for data subjects. Instead of each person managing their own data alone, a trust negotiates terms with platforms collectively. (Delacroix & Lawrence, 2019)
  • limit corporate concentration — support antitrust enforcement against platforms that have become too large to compete with. Monopoly power makes individual choice less meaningful.

C. institutionally we need

  • close the GDPR gap — the EU's General Data Protection Regulation is the most significant legal response to surveillance capitalism so far, but it regulates consent and transparency rather than the underlying business model. It was designed around active data sharing, not passive behavioral observation. Stronger legislation would restrict behavioral advertising directly, not just require a pop-up.
  • treat data like labor or property — if your behavioral data generates economic value, you should have rights over it similar to workers' rights or property rights. This would make extracting behavioral surplus without compensation legally comparable to unpaid labor.
  • require algorithmic transparency — companies should be required to disclose how recommendation systems work, who they target, and what behavioral data they use. Independent audits, not self-reporting.
  • public digital infrastructure — some scholars argue that platforms with this much social importance should be publicly owned or governed, like utilities. The internet was built on public investment; its most powerful layer is now privately owned.

D. by design we could

  • humane technology — designing platforms that work for users rather than against them. Chronological feeds instead of algorithmic ones, friction by design that slows the scroll rather than optimizing it, no autoplay, transparent recommendations. These are features platforms used to have before attention was fully optimized. (Tim Wu, Tristan Harris)
  • democratic rationalization — the argument that technology is not neutral; it embeds social choices. Different priorities could produce platforms that collect less, manipulate less, and give users genuine control. Surveillance capitalism is not how digital platforms have to work — it is how they were designed to work. (Andrew Feenberg)
  • alternative platforms — decentralized and federated social networks like Mastodon operate without behavioral advertising. They are smaller and less polished but they prove the model is possible.
